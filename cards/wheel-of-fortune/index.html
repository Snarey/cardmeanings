What does Wheel of Fortune mean?

Wheel of Fortune marks movement within a system larger than individual preference. Positions rise and fall around the wheel, while the symbols in its corners remain attentive. The card recognises luck and external conditions, but it also asks how knowledge affects a person's response to change.

In readings, it often appears when a situation is entering a new phase. The shift may be welcome or disruptive, but the existing arrangement is unlikely to remain fixed. The card encourages perspective: a present high is not permanent, and neither is a present low.

Upright meaning

Upright, Wheel of Fortune can indicate improving conditions, a fortunate opening or the moment when accumulated effort meets opportunity. Timing matters. An invitation, coincidence or external development may accelerate a process that previously felt static.

The card does not promise effortless success. It asks you to recognise movement and act while the opening exists. Flexibility will often prove more useful than trying to preserve an older plan unchanged.

Reversed meaning

Reversed, the wheel may turn against expectation. Delays, reversals or external events can expose how much a plan depended on favourable conditions. The response should focus on adaptation rather than treating every setback as personal failure.

The card can also reveal repetition. A pattern may feel like bad luck because its mechanism has not been examined. Identify what remains constant each time the cycle returns, including your own timing, assumptions and choices.

Symbols and imagery in Wheel of Fortune

The wheel

The central wheel presents life as movement, recurrence and shifting position. Its multiple rings suggest that visible events are connected with deeper systems.

The sphinx

The sphinx sits above the turning wheel with a sword. It represents stability, judgement and an intelligence that remains poised amid change.

The serpent and ascending figure

One form descends while another rises. Together they show loss and gain as simultaneous parts of the same motion.

The letters and symbols

The wheel carries letters that can be read in several sequences alongside alchemical signs. Their arrangement reinforces transformation and layered order.

The four winged creatures

The figures in the corners read books while surrounded by cloud. They suggest enduring witnesses, fixed signs or forms of knowledge that frame the wheel's instability.

A. E. Waite's original description of Wheel of Fortune

The following description is reproduced from A. E. Waite's The Pictorial Key to the Tarot. The original wording and punctuation have been retained.

In this symbol I have again followed the reconstruction of Éliphas Lévi, who has furnished several variants. It is legitimate, as I have intimated, to use Egyptian symbolism when this serves our purpose, provided that no theory of origin is implied therein. I have, however, presented Typhon in his serpent form.

The symbolism is, of course, not exclusively Egyptian, as the four Living Creatures of Ezekiel occupy the angles of the card, and the wheel itself follows other indications of Lévi in respect of Ezekiel's vision, as illustrative of the particular Tarot Key. With the French occultist, and in the design itself, the symbolic picture stands for the perpetual motion of a fluidic universe and for the flux of human life. The Sphinx is the equilibrium therein.

The transliteration of Taro as Rota is inscribed on the wheel, counterchanged with the letters of the Divine Name, to shew that Providence is implied through all. But this is the Divine intention within, and the similar intention without is exemplified by the four Living Creatures. Sometimes the sphinx is represented couchant on a pedestal above, which defrauds the symbolism by stultifying the essential idea of stability amidst movement.

Behind the general notion expressed in the symbol there lies the denial of chance and the fatality which is implied therein. It may be added that, from the days of Lévi onward, the occult explanations of this card are, even for occultism itself, of a singularly fatuous kind. It has been said to mean principle, fecundity, virile honour, ruling authority, etc. The findings of common fortune-telling are better than this on their own plane.

Waite's original divinatory meanings

Upright:

Destiny, fortune, success, elevation, luck, felicity.

Reversed:

Increase, abundance, superfluity.

Source: A. E. Waite, The Pictorial Key to the Tarot, first published in 1910, with illustrations by Pamela Colman Smith.

Wheel of Fortune in love and relationships

In relationships, Wheel of Fortune can mark a change in status, an unexpected meeting, renewed movement or circumstances that alter what is possible. The bond may be entering a different season rather than becoming simply better or worse.

Reversed, recurring relationship patterns may return. Instead of asking why this always happens, examine the point at which the same choices, silences or expectations enter the cycle.

Wheel of Fortune in work, money and creativity

At work, the card can indicate changing markets, leadership, timing or opportunity. Stay alert to developments outside the original plan. A useful opening may arrive through chance, but readiness determines whether it can be used.

Financially, expect variability and avoid assuming that recent gains or losses will continue indefinitely. Creatively, revisit old material when circumstances allow it to function in a new way.

Advice from Wheel of Fortune

Recognise what is changing and move with the useful part of the turn. Do not confuse a temporary position with a permanent identity, and study any pattern that keeps returning.

Questions to ask when Wheel of Fortune appears

  • What is changing whether I approve of it or not?
  • Where is timing creating an opening?
  • Which recurring pattern can now be understood differently?
