The Hanged Man

By Finbarre Snarey
10 July 2026

Reorientation

“I am rooted, but I flow.”

Virginia Woolf, The Waves

The Hanged Man at a glance

Finbarre’s interpretation: The Hanged Man represents suspension, surrender and the altered perspective that becomes possible when ordinary action stops.

What does The Hanged Man mean?

The Hanged Man is visibly constrained, yet his expression is calm and a halo surrounds his head. The image separates suspension from suffering. Action has stopped, but consciousness remains active. The card therefore concerns what can be learned when the normal drive to advance is interrupted.

It may describe a delay, a sacrifice, a period of uncertainty or a chosen withdrawal from conventional priorities. The value of the pause depends on whether it changes understanding. Waiting that produces no new perspective eventually becomes avoidance.

Upright meaning

Upright, The Hanged Man asks you to stop forcing the expected solution. A situation may need time, reversal of viewpoint or surrender of an assumption that previously organised the problem. The most useful action may be to suspend action temporarily.

The card can also indicate a conscious sacrifice. Time, comfort, status or an immediate result may be relinquished in service of something more important. The sacrifice should be chosen and intelligible, not demanded indefinitely without purpose.

Reversed meaning

Reversed, The Hanged Man can show that the pause has lost its meaning. A person may remain in limbo because every available choice has a cost, or present themselves as powerless while quietly refusing change.

It can also indicate sacrifice that is unrecognised, coerced or poorly directed. Ask what the delay is producing. If no insight, protection or strategic benefit remains, it may be time to end the suspension.

Symbols and imagery in The Hanged Man

The living wooden frame

Leaves grow from the structure, showing that suspension occurs within life rather than death. The apparent instrument of punishment is also a living tree.

The halo

The bright nimbus indicates illumination or altered consciousness. It shifts attention from the body's position to the mind's experience.

The crossed legs

The legs form a distinctive hooked shape while the arms create another triangle behind the body. The geometry makes the figure appear composed rather than chaotic.

The calm face

The expression contains no visible agony. This detail is central to reading the card as willing suspension, entrancement or spiritual reversal.

The blue and red clothing

The colours combine cool reflection with active vitality. The body is still, but energy has not disappeared.

A. E. Waite's original description of The Hanged Man

The following description is reproduced from A. E. Waite's The Pictorial Key to the Tarot. The original wording and punctuation have been retained.

The gallows from which he is suspended forms a Tau cross, while the figure, from the position of the legs, forms a fylfot cross. There is a nimbus about the head of the seeming martyr. It should be noted (1) that the tree of sacrifice is living wood, with leaves thereon; (2) that the face expresses deep entrancement, not suffering; (3) that the figure, as a whole, suggests life in suspension, but life and not death. It is a card of profound significance, but all the significance is veiled.

One of his editors suggests that Éliphas Lévi did not know the meaning, which is unquestionable, nor did the editor himself. It has been called falsely a card of martyrdom, a card of prudence, a card of the Great Work, a card of duty; but we may exhaust all published interpretations and find only vanity. I will say very simply on my own part that it expresses the relation, in one of its aspects, between the Divine and the Universe.

He who can understand that the story of his higher nature is imbedded in this symbolism will receive intimations concerning a great awakening that is possible, and will know that after the sacred Mystery of Death there is a glorious Mystery of Resurrection.

Waite's original divinatory meanings

Upright:

Wisdom, circumspection, discernment, trials, sacrifice, intuition, divination, prophecy.

Reversed:

Selfishness, the crowd, body politic.

Source: A. E. Waite, The Pictorial Key to the Tarot, first published in 1910, with illustrations by Pamela Colman Smith.

The Hanged Man in love and relationships

In relationships, The Hanged Man can indicate a pause, a change of perspective or a period in which one or both people must reconsider expectations. Space may be useful if it creates insight and is not used as indefinite emotional suspension.

Reversed, it can show waiting for someone who will not decide, repeatedly sacrificing your needs or remaining attached to potential rather than present behaviour. Ask what the waiting has actually changed.

The Hanged Man in work, money and creativity

At work, the card may signal a delayed project, sabbatical, strategic pause or the need to abandon a familiar method. A problem may become solvable only when the original measure of success is reconsidered.

Financially, avoid rushed decisions during uncertainty, but set a point for review. Creatively, turn the material around, change medium or allow an unfinished idea to rest until its hidden structure becomes visible.

Advice from The Hanged Man

Pause with purpose. Release the assumption that keeps reproducing the same view, and decide what insight or value would make the sacrifice worthwhile.

Questions to ask when The Hanged Man appears

  • What can be seen only from a reversed position?
  • What am I choosing to sacrifice, and for what?
  • Has this pause produced insight or merely postponed action?
