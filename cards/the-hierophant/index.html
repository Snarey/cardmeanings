What does The Hierophant mean?

The Hierophant concerns knowledge that has been organised, authorised and passed between generations. Unlike the High Priestess, whose wisdom remains private and inward, this figure presides over public doctrine, recognised ritual and an established hierarchy.

The card can represent religion, education, professional accreditation, marriage, cultural tradition or any system that tells a group how things are properly done. Its central question is not whether tradition is good or bad, but whether a particular structure still carries meaning and serves the people who inherit it.

Upright meaning

Upright, The Hierophant can point to a trustworthy teacher, a formal course, a ceremony or a body of practice that provides useful orientation. It may be wise to learn the rules before attempting to change them. Shared language and ritual can create belonging and preserve hard-won knowledge.

The card also favours explicit values. A group, partnership or institution may need to state what it stands for and how those principles are enacted, rather than relying on vague assumptions.

Reversed meaning

Reversed, The Hierophant can show a structure whose authority exceeds its wisdom. Rules may survive because they benefit the hierarchy, not because they remain useful. A teacher may demand deference, or a community may punish questions that expose contradiction.

The reversal can also mark a productive break from convention. Independent study, alternative ceremony or a personally chosen code may be necessary. Rebellion becomes meaningful when it creates a better practice, not merely an opposite posture.

Symbols and imagery in The Hierophant

The triple crown

The layered crown establishes religious and institutional authority. It suggests a hierarchy that connects different levels of doctrine or office.

The raised hand

The gesture of blessing distinguishes what is openly communicated from what remains concealed. It also mirrors, but differs from, the Magician's directing gesture.

The crossed keys

The keys suggest access, permission and the authority to open or close a sacred domain. They also represent the power held by an institution's gatekeepers.

The two kneeling figures

The ministers show teaching as a relationship between office and followers. Their presence raises questions about initiation, obedience and transmission.

The floral garments

The roses and lilies repeat imagery from The Magician, but here the symbols are worn by followers within an organised system rather than cultivated by a solitary adept.

A. E. Waite's original description of The Hierophant

The following description is reproduced from A. E. Waite's The Pictorial Key to the Tarot. The original wording and punctuation have been retained.

He wears the triple crown and is seated between two pillars, but they are not those of the Temple which is guarded by the High Priestess. In his left hand he holds a sceptre terminating in the triple cross, and with his right hand he gives the well-known ecclesiastical sign which is called that of esotericism, distinguishing between the manifest and concealed part of doctrine. It is noticeable in this connexion that the High Priestess makes no sign.

At his feet are the crossed keys, and two priestly ministers in albs kneel before him. He has been usually called the Pope, which is a particular application of the more general office that he symbolizes. He is the ruling power of external religion, as the High Priestess is the prevailing genius of the esoteric, withdrawn power. The proper meanings of this card have suffered woeful admixture from nearly all hands. Grand Orient says truly that the Hierophant is the power of the keys, exoteric orthodox doctrine, and the outer side of the life which leads to the doctrine; but he is certainly not the prince of occult doctrine, as another commentator has suggested.

He is rather the summa totius theologiæ, when it has passed into the utmost rigidity of expression; but he symbolizes also all things that are righteous and sacred on the manifest side. As such, he is the channel of grace belonging to the world of institution as distinct from that of Nature, and he is the leader of salvation for the human race at large.

He is the order and the head of the recognized hierarchy, which is the reflection of another and greater hierarchic order; but it may so happen that the pontiff forgets the significance of this his symbolic state and acts as if he contained within his proper measures all that his sign signifies or his symbol seeks to shew forth. He is not, as it has been thought, philosophy, except on the theological side; he is not inspiration; and he is not religion, although he is a mode of its expression.

Waite's original divinatory meanings

Upright:

Marriage, alliance, captivity, servitude; by another account, mercy and goodness; inspiration; the man to whom the Querent has recourse.

Reversed:

Society, good understanding, concord, overkindness, weakness.

Source: A. E. Waite, The Pictorial Key to the Tarot, first published in 1910, with illustrations by Pamela Colman Smith.

The Hierophant in love and relationships

In relationships, The Hierophant can indicate commitment, marriage, shared faith or a bond shaped by cultural and family expectations. It asks whether the couple's values are genuinely shared and how those values will be expressed in practice.

Reversed, it may indicate resistance to conventional partnership, conflict with family expectations or a relationship following rules that no longer fit. The answer may be a consciously designed commitment rather than either automatic conformity or reflexive rejection.

The Hierophant in work, money and creativity

At work, the card points to training, professional standards, institutional knowledge, mentorship and formal approval. Following an established procedure may save time, especially where regulation or safety matters.

Reversed, it may expose bureaucratic habits that block useful work. Creatively, study tradition closely enough to understand what you are adapting. Financially, seek qualified advice where the issue genuinely requires it, but distinguish expertise from status.

Advice from The Hierophant

Identify the tradition, rule or teacher shaping the decision. Keep what carries tested value, question what demands obedience without explanation, and make your commitments explicit.

Questions to ask when The Hierophant appears

  • Which inherited practice still serves a real purpose?
  • Who has been authorised to define what is correct, and why?
  • What values should this commitment make visible?
