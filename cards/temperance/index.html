What does Temperance mean?

Temperance is not static balance. The figure continually pours between two cups, suggesting an active process of testing, transferring and adjusting. One foot rests on land and the other in water, connecting practical reality with emotional or spiritual depth.

The card often appears during recovery, collaboration or any situation in which different needs must coexist. Its solution is rarely all-or-nothing. Progress comes through proportion, sequencing and the creation of a third possibility from elements that initially seem separate.

Upright meaning

Upright, Temperance favours gradual improvement, compromise and sustainable pacing. It can indicate healing after disruption, the blending of skills or the successful coordination of people with different approaches.

The card also supports moderation, but not colourless self-denial. It asks what amount, rhythm or mixture enables life to continue well. Small adjustments made consistently may be more effective than a dramatic correction.

Reversed meaning

Reversed, Temperance can show excess, poor fit or an unstable mixture. Competing priorities may be combined without enough time, resources or shared purpose. The result feels muddy rather than integrated.

It may also indicate impatience with recovery or a cycle of overdoing and compensating. Instead of seeking perfect balance immediately, identify the ingredient or behaviour that repeatedly pushes the system out of range.

Symbols and imagery in Temperance

The two cups

The liquid moves between separate vessels in an apparently impossible flow. The image represents exchange, adaptation and the creation of continuity between distinct forms.

One foot on land and one in water

The stance connects material and emotional domains. It suggests participation in both without being overwhelmed by either.

The path and distant crown

A clear path leads towards mountains and a radiant crown. Integration in the foreground becomes part of a longer movement towards purpose or illumination.

The triangle within the square

The emblem on the robe combines a triangle and square, often read as spirit within matter. It reinforces the card's concern with bringing different orders into relationship.

The irises

The flowers near the water introduce beauty and living growth. Their presence suggests that harmony produces more than neutrality; it supports flourishing.

A. E. Waite's original description of Temperance

The following description is reproduced from A. E. Waite's The Pictorial Key to the Tarot. The original wording and punctuation have been retained.

A winged angel, with the sign of the sun upon his forehead and on his breast the square and triangle of the septenary. I speak of him in the masculine sense, but the figure is neither male nor female. It is held to be pouring the essences of life from chalice to chalice. It has one foot upon the earth and one upon waters, thus illustrating the nature of the essences.

A direct path goes up to certain heights on the verge of the horizon, and above there is a great light, through which a crown is seen vaguely. Hereof is some part of the Secret of Eternal Life, as it is possible to man in his incarnation. All the conventional emblems are renounced herein.

So also are the conventional meanings, which refer to changes in the seasons, perpetual movement of life and even the combination of ideas. It is, moreover, untrue to say that the figure symbolizes the genius of the sun, though it is the analogy of solar light, realized in the third part of our human triplicity. It is called Temperance fantastically, because, when the rule of it obtains in our consciousness, it tempers, combines and harmonises the psychic and material natures.

Under that rule we know in our rational part something of whence we came and whither we are going.

Waite's original divinatory meanings

Upright:

Economy, moderation, frugality, management, accommodation.

Reversed:

Things connected with churches, religions, sects, the priesthood, sometimes even the priest who will marry the Querent; also disunion, unfortunate combinations, competing interests.

Source: A. E. Waite, The Pictorial Key to the Tarot, first published in 1910, with illustrations by Pamela Colman Smith.

Temperance in love and relationships

Temperance can indicate compatibility built through adjustment rather than perfect sameness. A relationship may be healing, finding a shared rhythm or learning how different temperaments can work together.

Reversed, it can reveal repeated extremes, incompatible expectations or one person doing all the adapting. Compromise is useful only when both people remain present in the resulting arrangement.

Temperance in work, money and creativity

At work, Temperance favours coordination, mediation, gradual reform and roles that combine different disciplines. It is a strong card for editing, production and any process requiring many parts to function as one.

Financially, it supports measured spending and recovery through consistent adjustment. Creatively, combine influences carefully enough that the result becomes a new work rather than a collection of borrowed parts.

Advice from Temperance

Adjust the mixture rather than abandoning the whole process. Work with proportion, pace and sequence, and allow integration enough time to become stable.

Questions to ask when Temperance appears

  • Which elements need combining rather than choosing between?
  • What small adjustment would make this more sustainable?
  • Is compromise creating a workable whole or erasing one side?
