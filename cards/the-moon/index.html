The Moon

By Finbarre Snarey
10 July 2026

What does The Moon mean?

The Moon illuminates, but only by reflected light. The path is visible between two towers, yet the landscape is populated by instinctive creatures and something unfamiliar emerging from the water. The card describes perception when evidence is incomplete and imagination fills the gaps.

This can be a fertile state for dreams, art and intuition, but a difficult one for firm conclusions. The Moon asks you to continue carefully, verify what can be verified and take emotional reactions seriously without treating each reaction as literal proof.

Upright meaning

Upright, The Moon may indicate confusion, mixed signals, hidden influences or a period when the destination is not yet clear. Anxiety can intensify because uncertainty gives fear room to create its own narrative.

The card also supports engagement with dreams, memory and symbolic thought. Notice recurring images and bodily responses, then test their relevance. Intuition is most useful when it opens a question rather than closes investigation.

Reversed meaning

Reversed, The Moon can indicate that confusion is lifting. Information becomes available, a deception is exposed or an anxiety is recognised as a response to an older experience rather than the present situation.

It can also show deeper entanglement in denial or fantasy. A person may insist that uncertainty has been resolved while continuing to avoid evidence. Clarity should be measured by what can now be named, checked and acted upon.

Symbols and imagery in The Moon

The moon's changing face

A profile appears within the lunar disc, surrounded by both rays and falling drops. The image combines observation, reflection and a world affected by indirect light.

The dog and wolf

The domesticated and wild animals respond together. They suggest different forms of instinct becoming vocal in the presence of uncertainty.

The crayfish

The creature rises from deep water at the start of the path. It represents primitive material, fear or awareness emerging slowly from the unconscious.

The two towers

The matching towers frame the exit from familiar territory. They mark a threshold into a landscape that cannot be fully surveyed.

The winding path

The road continues beyond sight, reminding the reader that uncertainty is navigated step by step rather than resolved from the starting point.

A. E. Waite's original description of The Moon

The following description is reproduced from A. E. Waite's The Pictorial Key to the Tarot. The original wording and punctuation have been retained.

The distinction between this card and some of the conventional types is that the moon is increasing on what is called the side of mercy, to the right of the observer. It has sixteen chief and sixteen secondary rays. The card represents life of the imagination apart from life of the spirit. The path between the towers is the issue into the unknown. The dog and wolf are the fears of the natural mind in the presence of that place of exit, when there is only reflected light to guide it.

The last reference is a key to another form of symbolism. The intellectual light is a reflection and beyond it is the unknown mystery which it cannot shew forth. It illuminates our animal nature, types of which are represented below, the dog, the wolf and that which comes up out of the deeps, the nameless and hideous tendency which is lower than the savage beast. It strives to attain manifestation, symbolized by crawling from the abyss of water to the land, but as a rule it sinks back whence it came.

The face of the mind directs a calm gaze upon the unrest below; the dew of thought falls; the message is: Peace, be still; and it may be that there shall come a calm upon the animal nature, while the abyss beneath shall cease from giving up a form.

Waite's original divinatory meanings

Upright:

Hidden enemies, danger, calumny, darkness, terror, deception, occult forces, error.

Reversed:

Instability, inconstancy, silence, lesser degrees of deception and error.

Source: A. E. Waite, The Pictorial Key to the Tarot, first published in 1910, with illustrations by Pamela Colman Smith.

The Moon in love and relationships

The Moon can indicate uncertainty, projection or a relationship in which not everything has been made explicit. Strong feelings may be present, but clarity about intention and behaviour is limited. Avoid making permanent decisions from a temporary emotional fog where possible.

Reversed, misunderstandings may begin to clear or hidden information may surface. It can also show reassurance being sought repeatedly because the underlying anxiety has not been addressed.

The Moon in work, money and creativity

At work, The Moon warns against rumours, vague instructions and decisions based on atmosphere rather than documented facts. Ask specific questions and preserve records while the situation remains unclear.

Financially, avoid opaque schemes and check assumptions. Creatively, the card is rich territory for dream, myth and ambiguity, but give the imaginative material enough form that an audience can enter it.

Advice from The Moon

Move slowly enough to verify the path. Record impressions, check facts and avoid treating either fear or hope as complete evidence while visibility remains limited.

Questions to ask when The Moon appears

  • What is known, and what has imagination supplied?
  • Which fear belongs to the present situation and which comes from an older one?
  • What is the next verifiable step along the path?
