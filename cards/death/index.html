What does Death mean?

Death is one of the most frequently misunderstood tarot cards. In most readings, it does not predict literal death. It represents an ending that changes the structure of life: a role, relationship, belief, habit or phase can no longer continue in its previous form.

The card is unsentimental because endings affect everyone in the scene, regardless of status. Yet the rising sun and living rose indicate that change is not equivalent to annihilation. Something passes, and that passage creates the conditions for another state.

Upright meaning

Upright, Death asks for honest recognition that a cycle has completed. Continuing to invest in the old form may prolong pain without restoring life. Grief, uncertainty and practical disruption can be part of a necessary transition.

The card can also indicate decisive renewal. Once an ending is accepted, energy previously tied to maintenance becomes available. Transformation is rarely neat, but it can be clarifying.

Reversed meaning

Reversed, Death often shows resistance to an inevitable or overdue ending. A person may remain attached to identity, familiarity or sunk cost even when the situation is no longer alive. The attempt to avoid loss can turn transition into stagnation.

It may also indicate that an ending has occurred externally but not internally. Habits, expectations or emotional bonds continue to organise life after the formal change. Integration requires acknowledging what is gone and what has not yet been released.

Symbols and imagery in Death

The skeleton

The rider reduces identity to the structure that remains beneath social difference. The armour makes death impersonal and resistant to negotiation.

The white horse

The pale horse carries the transformation forward with calm momentum. White can suggest purification, clarity or the neutrality of natural process.

The black banner and white rose

The flag combines darkness with a living five-petalled rose. The image holds ending and renewal within the same emblem.

The fallen figures

King, child and maiden respond differently, while the cleric faces the rider. The scene shows that change reaches every rank and stage of life.

The rising sun

Between distant pillars, the sun appears on the horizon. It presents death as passage towards another state rather than a closed void.

A. E. Waite's original description of Death

The following description is reproduced from A. E. Waite's The Pictorial Key to the Tarot. The original wording and punctuation have been retained.

The veil or mask of life is perpetuated in change, transformation and passage from lower to higher, and this is more fitly represented in the rectified Tarot by one of the apocalyptic visions than by the crude notion of the reaping skeleton. Behind it lies the whole world of ascent in the spirit. The mysterious horseman moves slowly, bearing a black banner emblazoned with the Mystic Rose, which signifies life. Between two pillars on the verge of the horizon there shines the sun of immortality.

The horseman carries no visible weapon, but king and child and maiden fall before him, while a prelate with clasped hands awaits his end.

There should be no need to point out that the suggestion of death which I have made in connexion with the previous card is, of course, to be understood mystically, but this is not the case in the present instance.

The natural transit of man to the next stage of his being either is or may be one form of his progress, but the exotic and almost unknown entrance, while still in this life, into the state of mystical death is a change in the form of consciousness and the passage into a state to which ordinary death is neither the path nor gate. The existing occult explanations of the 13th card are, on the whole, better than usual, rebirth, creation, destination, renewal, and the rest.

Waite's original divinatory meanings

Upright:

End, mortality, destruction, corruption; also, for a man, the loss of a benefactor; for a woman, many contrarieties; for a maid, failure of marriage projects.

Reversed:

Inertia, sleep, lethargy, petrifaction, somnambulism; hope destroyed.

Source: A. E. Waite, The Pictorial Key to the Tarot, first published in 1910, with illustrations by Pamela Colman Smith.

Death in love and relationships

In relationships, Death can indicate the end of a bond, but it can also describe the death of an old dynamic within a continuing relationship. A partnership may need to change fundamentally rather than return to how it was.

Reversed, it can show an ending being denied, a former relationship still shaping current choices or fear of allowing a bond to evolve. Holding the same form may be more damaging than accepting transition.

Death in work, money and creativity

At work, Death may mark redundancy, departure, retirement, restructuring or a complete change of direction. It can also signal the deliberate ending of a project whose purpose has been fulfilled.

Financially, close obsolete commitments and plan for transition rather than expecting immediate replacement. Creatively, remove the version that no longer belongs, even when considerable effort has already been invested in it.

Advice from Death

Name what has ended. Allow grief and practical adjustment, then stop feeding a form that cannot return. Direct attention towards what the ending now makes possible.

Questions to ask when Death appears

  • What is no longer alive despite my effort to maintain it?
  • Which identity or expectation must be released?
  • What new condition becomes possible after this ending is accepted?
