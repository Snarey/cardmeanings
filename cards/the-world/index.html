What does The World mean?

The World is the final numbered card of the Major Arcana. The central figure moves within a complete wreath while the four corner beings echo those surrounding Wheel of Fortune. What was once turning beyond personal control has become a whole within which movement and stability coexist.

The card can indicate graduation, publication, travel, completion, recognition or the successful integration of a major life experience. It is not merely the stopping point. Completion changes the scale of possibility and creates the threshold from which another Fool's journey can begin.

Upright meaning

Upright, The World confirms that a significant cycle is complete or close enough to completion that its achievement can be recognised. Bring the work together, mark the transition and allow yourself to occupy the result.

The card may also point to travel, international connection or entry into a wider community. Skills and experiences that once felt separate can now function as an integrated whole.

Reversed meaning

Reversed, The World can show a final task, missing element or reluctance to declare completion. Perfectionism may keep a finished work private, or nostalgia may prevent movement beyond an identity that has served its purpose.

The reversal can also indicate closure without integration. An event has ended, but its meaning has not been absorbed. Review what has changed and decide what will be carried deliberately into the next cycle.

Symbols and imagery in The World

The wreath

The oval wreath encloses the central figure while remaining tied at top and bottom. It represents completion, protected space and a cycle held together.

The dancing figure

The figure is active rather than enthroned. Completion is presented as living movement and freedom within form, not frozen perfection.

The two wands

A wand is held in each hand, balancing power across the body. Their presence recalls The Magician while suggesting mastery that no longer depends on a table of separate tools.

The four winged beings

The human, eagle, lion and bull occupy the corners as they do in Wheel of Fortune. Here they frame a realised whole rather than a turning wheel.

The crossed sash

The flowing purple fabric curves around the figure and crosses the body. It introduces concealment, movement and a visual echo of the wreath's binding ribbons.

A. E. Waite's original description of The World

The following description is reproduced from A. E. Waite's The Pictorial Key to the Tarot. The original wording and punctuation have been retained.

As this final message of the Major Trumps is unchanged, and indeed unchangeable, in respect of its design, it has been partly described already regarding its deeper sense. It represents also the perfection and end of the Cosmos, the secret which is within it, the rapture of the universe when it understands itself in God. It is further the state of the soul in the consciousness of Divine Vision, reflected from the self-knowing spirit.

But these meanings are without prejudice to that which I have said concerning it on the material side.

It has more than one message on the macrocosmic side and is, for example, the state of the restored world when the law of manifestation shall have been carried to the highest degree of natural perfection. But it is perhaps more especially a story of the past, referring to that day when all was declared to be good, when the morning stars sang together and all the Sons of God shouted for joy.

One of the worst explanations concerning it is that the figure symbolizes the Magus when he has reached the highest degree of initiation; another account says that it represents the absolute, which is ridiculous. The figure has been said to stand for Truth, which is, however, more properly allocated to the seventeenth card. Lastly, it has been called the Crown of the Magi.

Waite's original divinatory meanings

Upright:

Assured success, recompense, voyage, route, emigration, flight, change of place.

Reversed:

Inertia, fixity, stagnation, permanence.

Source: A. E. Waite, The Pictorial Key to the Tarot, first published in 1910, with illustrations by Pamela Colman Smith.

The World in love and relationships

The World can indicate a relationship reaching fulfilment, a major shared milestone or a bond that allows both people to participate in a larger life. It may also mark peaceful completion when a relationship has achieved what it could.

Reversed, a final conversation, decision or practical step may remain outstanding. The relationship can feel almost complete while one unresolved issue prevents full transition.

The World in work, money and creativity

At work, The World supports completion, publication, qualification, international reach and recognition based on a substantial body of effort. Finish the final details and make the result accessible.

Financially, it can indicate the completion of a long plan or a move into a broader market. Creatively, assemble the parts into a definitive form rather than continuing to generate new fragments.

Advice from The World

Complete the final practical step and mark the achievement. Integrate what the cycle has taught you, then allow completion to open the next beginning rather than becoming a place to remain fixed.

Questions to ask when The World appears

  • What is complete even if it is not perfect?
  • Which final detail would allow genuine closure?
  • How has this cycle enlarged the world I can now enter?
