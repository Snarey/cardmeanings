The Sun

By Finbarre Snarey
10 July 2026

Radiance

What does The Sun mean?

The Sun replaces The Moon's reflected and uncertain light with direct illumination. The child is open, animated and unarmoured, while the white horse moves without visible restraint. The card presents vitality as a restored relationship between consciousness, body and environment.

In readings, The Sun is strongly favourable. It can indicate success, visibility, warmth, health, confidence or a truth becoming simple enough to act upon. Its optimism is not secrecy-free perfection. It is the ability to meet reality without needing it to remain hidden.

Upright meaning

Upright, The Sun supports celebration, recognition and direct communication. A project may succeed, uncertainty may resolve or energy may return after a difficult period. Share the result and allow achievement to be experienced rather than immediately converted into another task.

The card also favours simplicity. A situation that seemed complicated may become clear when the central fact is stated plainly. Confidence is strongest when it does not need exaggeration.

Reversed meaning

Reversed, The Sun usually retains some positive quality, but its expression may be delayed, reduced or difficult to receive. Success can arrive with exhaustion, joy can be overshadowed by worry or a person may distrust good news because disappointment feels more familiar.

The reversal can also indicate overconfidence, performance or too much exposure. Not everything needs to be made public simply because visibility is available. Protect the private conditions that keep vitality real.

Symbols and imagery in The Sun

The large sun

The sun dominates the card with alternating straight and wavering rays. It represents direct consciousness, warmth and an environment in which concealment is difficult.

The child

The child embodies openness, simplicity and renewed life. The absence of clothing or armour suggests confidence without defensive status.

The white horse

The horse carries instinctive energy in a calm, purified form. No saddle or reins are visible, reinforcing harmony rather than forced control.

The red banner

The banner introduces active life, celebration and public visibility. It moves freely above the child rather than functioning as a weapon or boundary.

The sunflowers and wall

The flowers turn towards the sun behind a low wall. They show cultivated life responding to illumination while the child moves beyond enclosure.

A. E. Waite's original description of The Sun

The following description is reproduced from A. E. Waite's The Pictorial Key to the Tarot. The original wording and punctuation have been retained.

The naked child mounted on a white horse and displaying a red standard has been mentioned already as the better symbolism connected with this card. It is the destiny of the Supernatural East and the great and holy light which goes before the endless procession of humanity, coming out from the walled garden of the sensitive life and passing on the journey home.

The card signifies, therefore, the transit from the manifest light of this world, represented by the glorious sun of earth, to the light of the world to come, which goes before aspiration and is typified by the heart of a child.

But the last allusion is again the key to a different form or aspect of the symbolism. The sun is that of consciousness in the spirit, the direct as the antithesis of the reflected light. The characteristic type of humanity has become a little child therein, a child in the sense of simplicity and innocence in the sense of wisdom. In that simplicity, he bears the seal of Nature and of Art; in that innocence, he signifies the restored world.

When the self-knowing spirit has dawned in the consciousness above the natural mind, that mind in its renewal leads forth the animal nature in a state of perfect conformity.

Waite's original divinatory meanings

Upright:

Material happiness, fortunate marriage, contentment.

Reversed:

The same in a lesser sense.

Source: A. E. Waite, The Pictorial Key to the Tarot, first published in 1910, with illustrations by Pamela Colman Smith.

The Sun in love and relationships

The Sun can indicate happiness, openness and a relationship that is able to exist publicly and honestly. It favours shared joy, children, celebration and the resolution of uncertainty through direct communication.

Reversed, a good relationship may be strained by unrealistic expectations, lack of privacy or difficulty enjoying what is present. It can also reveal optimism being used to avoid a necessary conversation.

The Sun in work, money and creativity

At work, The Sun supports recognition, successful launches, clear leadership and projects that benefit from visibility. Results are likely to be easier to explain and share than under more ambiguous cards.

Financially, it can indicate improvement or confidence, while warning against spending based on the assumption that good conditions cannot change. Creatively, make the central idea visible and remove complications that dilute its energy.

Advice from The Sun

State the truth plainly and let the positive result be real. Celebrate without exaggeration, and protect enough privacy that visible success remains connected to genuine vitality.

Questions to ask when The Sun appears

  • What has become clear enough to name directly?
  • Can I receive this success without immediately minimising it?
  • What simple action would let this vitality circulate?
