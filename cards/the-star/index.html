The Star

By Finbarre Snarey
10 July 2026

Replenishment

“We are all in the gutter, but some of us are looking at the stars.”

Oscar Wilde, Lady Windermere’s Fan

What does The Star mean?

The Star follows The Tower. After rupture, the scene becomes open, quiet and unguarded. The central figure pours water onto both land and pool, replenishing the environment without visible urgency. Hope here is not denial of what happened; it is the return of enough trust to participate in life again.

The card may indicate recovery, inspiration, honesty or a period in which direction becomes visible after confusion. Its promise is spacious rather than immediate. The future is possible, but it still needs care, time and embodied action.

Upright meaning

Upright, The Star supports healing, creative renewal and an honest sense of possibility. You may feel able to speak more openly, make longer plans or reconnect with a purpose that survived recent difficulty.

The card can also represent visibility and authenticity. Being seen may become restorative when the environment is safe enough and the self no longer needs to perform certainty. Share what is true, but retain appropriate boundaries.

Reversed meaning

Reversed, The Star can show depleted hope or a sense that renewal is available to everyone except you. Discouragement may narrow attention until supportive evidence is no longer noticed. This is not a command to become positive; it is an invitation to find one credible source of continuation.

The card can also warn against inspiration without grounding. A beautiful future image may soothe the present while no practical step supports it. Hope becomes stronger when attached to a routine, resource or relationship.

Symbols and imagery in The Star

The large eight-pointed star

The central star dominates the sky and is surrounded by seven smaller stars. It provides orientation and a stable point above the open landscape.

The two vessels

Water is poured into the pool and across the land. The figure replenishes inner and outer worlds rather than choosing one over the other.

The naked figure

Nudity indicates openness and a lack of defensive status. Unlike the figures in The Lovers, she is alone, suggesting restored relationship with the self and environment.

The bird and tree

A bird rests in the tree behind her. It introduces watchfulness, communication or spirit into an otherwise still scene.

One foot on water

The figure seems to balance partly upon the pool while kneeling on land. The position joins intuition with practical contact.

A. E. Waite's original description of The Star

The following description is reproduced from A. E. Waite's The Pictorial Key to the Tarot. The original wording and punctuation have been retained.

A great, radiant star of eight rays, surrounded by seven lesser stars, also of eight rays. The female figure in the foreground is entirely naked. Her left knee is on the land and her right foot upon the water. She pours Water of Life from two great ewers, irrigating sea and land. Behind her is rising ground and on the right a shrub or tree, whereon a bird alights. The figure expresses eternal youth and beauty.

The star is l'étoile flamboyante, which appears in Masonic symbolism, but has been confused therein. That which the figure communicates to the living scene is the substance of the heavens and the elements. It has been said truly that the mottoes of this card are “Waters of Life freely” and “Gifts of the Spirit.”

The summary of several tawdry explanations says that it is a card of hope. On other planes it has been certified as immortality and interior light. For the majority of prepared minds, the figure will appear as the type of Truth unveiled, glorious in undying beauty, pouring on the waters of the soul some part and measure of her priceless possession.

But she is in reality the Great Mother in the Kabalistic Sephira Binah, which is supernal Understanding, who communicates to the Sephiroth that are below in the measure that they can receive her influx.

Waite's original divinatory meanings

Upright:

Loss, theft, privation, abandonment; another reading says hope and bright prospects.

Reversed:

Arrogance, haughtiness, impotence.

Source: A. E. Waite, The Pictorial Key to the Tarot, first published in 1910, with illustrations by Pamela Colman Smith.

The Star in love and relationships

The Star can indicate emotional honesty, healing after conflict or a relationship in which each person can become less defended. It may also mark hope returning after loss, whether within an existing bond or before a new one begins.

Reversed, one person may feel unseen, discouraged or too exposed. Promises about the future need to be supported by consistent behaviour in the present.

The Star in work, money and creativity

At work, The Star favours long-term vision, public communication, purposeful projects and recovery after professional disruption. It can indicate renewed confidence or recognition that feels aligned rather than merely flattering.

Financially, rebuild gradually and distinguish credible improvement from wishful projection. Creatively, follow the idea that restores energy, then give it a repeatable practice.

Advice from The Star

Choose a hope that can survive contact with reality. Replenish both the inner life and the practical environment, and let small evidence of renewal count.

Questions to ask when The Star appears

  • What future can I now imagine that recently felt impossible?
  • Which source of renewal is already available?
  • How can hope be attached to one practical action?
