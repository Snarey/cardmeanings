The Hermit

By Finbarre Snarey
10 July 2026

Seclusion

“The unexamined life is not worth living.”

The Apology of Socrates

The Hermit at a glance

What does The Hermit mean?

The Hermit stands alone at a height, but his lantern is held outward. His solitude is therefore not merely retreat. It is a position from which hard-won understanding can become a signal to others. The card values distance from distraction, not permanent disappearance.

In a reading, The Hermit may indicate research, contemplation, spiritual practice, ageing, mentorship or the need to make a decision without crowd pressure. It can also describe a person whose guidance is useful because it comes from lived experience rather than status.

Upright meaning

Upright, The Hermit encourages a deliberate reduction of noise. Step back from other people's urgency, competing opinions and the demand for immediate performance. A period of private work may reveal what public discussion has obscured.

The card can also show readiness to seek or offer guidance. The right teacher does not demand imitation. They provide enough light for the next part of the path while allowing the learner to walk it themselves.

Reversed meaning

Reversed, The Hermit can indicate that useful solitude has become isolation. Reflection may be repeating the same thought without new evidence, or withdrawal may protect a person from challenge as much as from distraction.

It can also describe fear of being seen, mistrust of advice or concealed behaviour. The reversal asks whether privacy is helping understanding or simply postponing re-entry into the world.

Symbols and imagery in The Hermit

The lantern

The lantern contains a six-pointed star and illuminates only a limited area. It suggests guidance sufficient for the next step rather than total knowledge of the route.

The mountain

The elevated setting implies attainment, difficulty and distance from ordinary activity. The figure has reached a vantage point, but the landscape is cold and sparse.

The staff

The staff supports the traveller and marks practical experience. It also resembles the wand as a simple instrument of directed will.

The grey cloak

The muted garment withdraws the figure from display. It suggests seriousness, anonymity and a life temporarily stripped of distraction.

The bowed head

The Hermit's posture is reflective rather than triumphant. Wisdom is presented as attention and humility, not certainty performed for an audience.

A. E. Waite's original description of The Hermit

The following description is reproduced from A. E. Waite's The Pictorial Key to the Tarot. The original wording and punctuation have been retained.

The variation from the conventional models in this card is only that the lamp is not enveloped partially in the mantle of its bearer, who blends the idea of the Ancient of Days with the Light of the World. It is a star which shines in the lantern. I have said that this is a card of attainment, and to extend this conception the figure is seen holding up his beacon on an eminence.

Therefore the Hermit is not, as Court de Gebelin explained, a wise man in search of truth and justice; nor is he, as a later explanation proposes, an especial example of experience. His beacon intimates that “where I am, you also may be.”

It is further a card which is understood quite incorrectly when it is connected with the idea of occult isolation, as the protection of personal magnetism against admixture. This is one of the frivolous renderings which we owe to Éliphas Lévi. It has been adopted by the French Order of Martinism and some of us have heard a great deal of the Silent and Unknown Philosophy enveloped by his mantle from the knowledge of the profane. In true Martinism, the significance of the term Philosophe inconnu was of another order. It did not refer to the intended concealment of the Instituted Mysteries, much less of their substitutes, but, like the card itself, to the truth that the Divine Mysteries secure their own protection from those who are unprepared.

Waite's original divinatory meanings

Upright:

Prudence, circumspection; also and especially treason, dissimulation, roguery, corruption.

Reversed:

Concealment, disguise, policy, fear, unreasoned caution.

Source: A. E. Waite, The Pictorial Key to the Tarot, first published in 1910, with illustrations by Pamela Colman Smith.

The Hermit in love and relationships

The Hermit can indicate a need for personal space, a quiet relationship or a period of reflection before further commitment. It may also describe a bond shaped by maturity, mentorship or a significant age difference.

Reversed, it can show emotional unavailability, loneliness within a relationship or withdrawal used to avoid communication. Space should have a purpose and, where possible, a clear route back into contact.

The Hermit in work, money and creativity

At work, The Hermit supports research, specialist practice, independent work and consultation with an experienced adviser. A quieter period may produce better judgement than continuous visibility.

Financially, it favours caution and review, but warns against refusing all opportunity through fear. Creatively, protect time for concentrated work, then decide how the result will eventually meet its audience.

Advice from The Hermit

Step away long enough to hear your own judgement. Seek a trustworthy guide where needed, and make sure solitude remains a route to understanding rather than a permanent defence.

Questions to ask when The Hermit appears

  • What becomes clearer when outside opinion is removed?
  • Is this solitude restorative or avoidant?
  • What light can I offer without pretending to know the whole path?
